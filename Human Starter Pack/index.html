Design Standard & DNA

Clothing Aesthetics Playbook

Inspired by the highly functional Power Design standard, this guide represents the architectural blueprint of our clothing storefront collections.

Section 1: Visual Composition & Layout
01

The Dual-Image Product Card

Every product card in a catalog grid MUST display a flat-lay (garment-only) photo on load, and seamlessly morph/cross-fade (`transition: opacity 250ms cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1)`) into a model fit/editorial photo on pointer hover.

Humans buy clothing to see how it fits a body, but browse to see the product details. Combining both reduces cognitive friction.
Visual Catalog
02

Whitespace-to-Content Ratio (Grid Breathing)

Product grids must maintain a minimum whitespace ratio of 45%. This is measured as the ratio of pixels containing only the background canvas color relative to the total viewport area. Grid gaps/gutters must be at least 32px.

High-end fashion is defined by breathing room. High content density signals "bargain bin" discount shopping; low density signals premium exclusivity.
Layout
03

Portrait Editorial Aspect Ratios

All product catalog photography must maintain a strict 3:4 portrait aspect ratio (`aspect-ratio: 3 / 4`). Technical or macro detail cards may use a 1:1 square ratio (`aspect-ratio: 1 / 1`). Product photos must never be horizontal, landscape, or mixed aspect ratios within a single grid.

Human bodies are vertical. 3:4 is the gold standard for luxury lookbooks, maximizing screen height to present full garments.
Photography
04

Asymmetric Grid Spacing (The 62/38 Split)

Double-wide promotional blocks or hero sliders must split the image and text using the Golden Ratio (61.8% image / 38.2% background content) or a Rule-of-Thirds split (66.6% / 33.3%). Never split a hero banner 50/50.

Symmetrical splits are static and unengaging; asymmetric weight creates kinetic energy that pulls the eye across the canvas.
Layout Grid
05

Hairline Border Simplicity

Grid lines, cards, and input borders must be hairline-thin (exactly 1px solid) and colored using neutral-soft opacities (`rgba(0,0,0,0.06)` or `rgba(255,255,255,0.08)`). Thick or high-contrast borders must be avoided.

Borders exist to group, not to draw attention. Dark lines choke the light out of streetwear and lookbook photography.
UI Borders
Section 2: Color, Light, & Contrast
06

Canvas-Photography Color Sync

The page background canvas color must match the ambient lighting temperature of the catalog photography. Cold/high-contrast studios require clean grays (`#F5F5F7` to `#FAFAFA`). Warm/grainy lookbooks require cream/parchment palettes (`#FDFBF7` to `#F7F4EB`).

If the photo background is warm and the page is stark blue-white, the photo will look dirty, creating a jarring, unpolished edge.
Color System
07

The 60-30-10 Color Scheme System

Limit the UI color palette to exactly three roles: 60% dominant background neutral (canvas), 30% primary typography and structural lines (ink), and exactly 10% (or less) of a single saturated brand accent color reserved strictly for action CTAs (Add to Cart, Checkout).

Keeping color highly sparse focuses attention on the clothing colors themselves. The brand accent becomes an interactive guidepost.
Color System
08

Meaning Beyond Hue

Color must never be the sole indicator of status (e.g. sale badges, stock warning). Pair red/green badges with high-contrast text or explicit labels (e.g., standard text tag "LOW STOCK" in uppercase tracking +0.1em).

Matches WCAG 1.4.1 requirements and ensures the interface is accessible to color-blind shoppers (~8% of male users).
Accessibility
09

Projector/Mobile-Resilient Contrast

All body typography and size indicators must maintain a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 (AA standard) against the canvas. Primary headers should aim for 7:1 (AAA standard) to resist ambient light wash-out on low-end screens.

Clothing sites are frequently browsed on mobile devices outdoors under strong sunlight; high contrast guarantees readability anywhere.
Accessibility
Section 3: Typography & Text Layout
10

The Two-Family Typography Cap

A storefront must utilize a maximum of two font families: 1 Editorial Serif (or Heavy Grotesque Display) for headlines + 1 highly readable Sans-serif (or Monospace) for body copy, size selectors, pricing, and specs.

Multiple type styles distract the eye, making the website look disjointed. Variable weights within two families offer plenty of hierarchy.
Typography
11

Display vs. Functional Letter-Spacing

Big display headings (≥40px) must have negative letter-spacing applied (`letter-spacing: -0.02em` to `-0.04em`). Small labels, price tags, and uppercase metadata (≤12px) must open their tracking (`letter-spacing: 0.1em` to `0.2em`).

Tightening display type makes letters lock together into cohesive wordmarks; opening small labels keeps them highly legible at small sizes.
Typography
12

Line-Height Bounds for Readability

Body text (14px–18px) must use a line-height between 1.4 and 1.6. Large display headings (≥32px) must clamp line-height tightly between 1.05 and 1.2 to prevent sprawling lines.

Too tight body text merges lines into dark stripes; too loose headline text disconnects the words from the same phrase.
Typography
13

Technical Specs Monospace Overlay

Any technical performance garment specifications (e.g. GSM weight, fabric composite, hydrophobic coatings) must be rendered in a strict monospace typeface, downsized to exactly 10px–11px, capitalized, and tracked by +0.15em.

Monospace evokes diagnostics and utilitarian precision, visually reinforcing the technical features of technical/outdoors streetwear.
Typography Specs
Section 4: Interaction & Micro-UX
14

Hover Size Chips Display

Apparel catalog cards must reveal available size chips (`S`, `M`, `L`, `XL`) instantly on card pointer hover using a smooth Y-axis translation (`transform: translateY(-8px)` with opacity fade-in over 200ms).

Allows immediate check of availability without forcing the shopper to click and wait for a separate product page load.
Micro-UX
15

Soft Out-of-Stock Size Chips

Never delete or hide sold-out sizes. Style them at 35% opacity with a diagonal 1px slash strike-through. Signals authentic high-demand inventory.

Tells the user that the size was designed and existed, and allows them to sign up for notifications instead of assuming it was never offered.
Micro-UX
16

Dynamic Glassmorphic Navigation Header

Sticky navigation headers must transition from 100% transparent on page load/hero overlap to a frosted glass state (`backdrop-filter: blur(12px) saturate(180%)`, background color opacity between 70% and 80%) once scrolled ≥80px from top.

Retains the full-bleed beauty of visual hero imagery while keeping navigation highly accessible and legible over dark/busy catalog grids.
Header UX
17

Slide-Out Utility Side-Cart (The Drawer)

Clicking "Cart" must trigger a slide-out cart drawer occupying exactly the right 400px–420px of the screen, paired with a dark backdrop mask (`rgba(0,0,0,0.5)`) fading in over 300ms. Never redirect to a standalone cart page.

Keeps the checkout flow inline. Seamless shopping transitions increase conversion rates by keeping the customer on the product grid.
Cart UX
18

The Kinetic Brand Loop (Infinite Marquee)

Horizontal text marquee loops must run at a constant speed between 30 and 45 seconds per cycle, and must be hardware-accelerated using standard CSS transform variables (`transform: translate3d(0,0,0)`).

Kinetic motion draws attention, but too fast motion causes reading fatigue. A gentle, sweeping pace keeps the site feeling alive.
Animation
19

Dynamic Cart Badge Scale Pop

When a product is added to the cart, the numeric item-count badge must perform a spring scale scaling up to exactly 1.25× before snapping back to 1.0× over exactly 200ms (`transform: scale(1.25)` to `scale(1)`).

Provides satisfying, immediate tactile feedback that confirms a system reaction without using interruptive popup alerts.
Tactile Feedback
20

Seamless Form Inputs & Validation

Checkout and drop email inputs must use standard CSS `:user-valid` and `:user-invalid` selectors. Green checkmark indicators or light validation glows must only trigger after focus blur—never flash error messages while the user is actively typing.

Eliminates early-typing stress and form friction, providing a smooth, high-end shopping experience.
Validation UX

Product Detail Page Guidelines

Apparel Product Detail Pages (PDP) are not transactional item listings—they are digital showrooms. Our blueprint balances visuals with structured tech specifications, search authority, and micro-UX details.

1. The 6-Step Visual Storyboard Gallery

To replicate the high-end boutique feel, every product catalog gallery follows a strict image sequence representing the buyer's psychological journey. All photography must strictly adhere to a **3:4 Portrait Aspect Ratio** (`aspect-ratio: 3 / 4`).

01 THE HOOK

On-Model Front Shot

Instant persona hook, style fit, and emotional resonance. The model represents the customer's aspirational community.

Execution: Front-facing styled model shot. The posture Projects relaxed confidence—never stiff. Avoid flat-lays as primary entry photos.
No studio flats
02 STRUCTURE

On-Model Back Shot

Establish visual trust and reveal structural construction details, seams, graphics, and neck labels.

Execution: Clear on-model back view. This is critical for streetwear where major graphics are frequently printed on the back.
Seams & print details
03 THE STYLE

Layered Outfit Shot

Increase average order value (AOV) and demonstrate complete contextual garment styling guides.

Execution: Full body on-model shot showcasing the item styled inside a layered outfit (e.g. vintage cap, loafers, knitted cardigan).
Loafers & cardigans
04 FABRIC TRUST

Macro Textural Zoom

Replace the in-store physical touch experience. Verifies fabric density and build quality.

Execution: Macro zoom shot highlighting stitching grains, collar ribs, print ink density, or embroidery details.
240 GSM organic slubs
05 SILHOUETTE

Profile & Volume

Define fabric weight, drape, sleeve fall, and total boxiness under slight motion.

Execution: Side or three-quarters view of the model standing, turning, or walking. Shows how the boxy retro fit drops off the shoulder.
3/4 motion turn
06 THE LIFE

Ambient Lifestyle

Deepen brand storytelling and reinforce cultural authenticity in an organic environment.

Execution: Informal, cinematically graded lifestyle shot in an authentic urban setting (brownstone stairs, vintage reading lounge).
Warm vintage grading

4. Conversion-Focused Micro-UX Details

Size Chips & Soft Out-of-Stock strike-through

Do not hide sold-out sizes. Style them at 35% opacity with a diagonal 1px slash strike-through. This confirms product demand and lets users click the size to open a notification modal instead of creating dead interface space.

Mobile Sticky ATC (Add To Cart) Bar

On mobile screens, once the user scrolls past the main purchase card, a sticky floating bar slides up from the bottom displaying thumbnail, price, size, and CTA button to bypass scrolling friction.

Inline Slide-Out Cart Drawer

Adding to cart must slide out a right-side glassmorphic cart drawer pairing with a dim mask layer. Standard stand-alone cart redirects break shopping momentum and drop conversion rates by up to 22%.
